How To Get Paid for being bad at comedy

The economics of stand-up comedy in Indian cities are, by every reasonable measure, a scam. Not on the audiences. Not on the venues. On the comics themselves.

By Anant Tyagi Bombay · 5 min read

The economics of stand-up comedy in Indian cities are, by every reasonable measure, a scam. Not on the audiences. Not on the venues. On the comics themselves, although they do not know it yet, and they would not believe you if you told them.

Here is how it works. A young man in Delhi or Bombay, drunk one Tuesday night, says something at a friend's house party that gets a laugh. His equally drunk friends, in the way friends do, tell him he is hilarious. He believes them. The next morning, he is no longer a finance analyst or a marketing intern or a college student. He is a comedian. He emails three open-mic venues asking to perform. They say yes. There is one detail he does not yet understand. The venues, in many cases, charge him to be on stage.

He pays them. He pays them so that he can workshop material he has not yet written, in front of an audience that has paid separately to be there. He gets seven minutes. He uses four of them. He bombs in three of those four, and lands exactly one joke, which was the joke his friends laughed at three weeks ago, only it does not land the same way in front of strangers. He does not understand why. He blames the room.

He goes back next week. He writes more jokes. He pays the venue again. He bombs again. The few that land, he stretches into bits. He starts telling himself that this is craft, this is the work, this is what every great comedian has done. He is not entirely wrong. But he is missing the thing that the great comedians did, which is read for ten years before going on stage, and write five hours a day, and watch their old sets back, and cut.

Most of the guys at most of the open mics do none of those things. They show up. They tell their unfinished material. They get paid, sometimes, after they have built up enough name recognition that the venue agrees to flip the deal. They get drunk. They smoke cheap weed. They argue with each other on Instagram about who copied whose bit, even though the bits in question are almost identical to bits that were on Comedy Central in 2014.

Persistence is the actual skill

Here is the part that nobody likes to admit. The guys who keep doing this, who go from paying venues to being paid by venues, are not necessarily the funny ones. They are the ones who hustled hardest, who promoted themselves loudest, who became their own sales arm, who learned to read a room and adjust. Some of them are funny. Most of them are not. And it does not matter.

This is the thing that breaks people who come to comedy from a writer's background. They assume the funniest people will rise. They will not. The most persistent and most self-promotional will rise. The funniest people, the ones who write three brilliant minutes and refuse to do any social media, will end up working day jobs forever. Comedy is the only craft I know of where the median performer is below the median of the audience that pays to see them, and the system has worked out a way to keep them employed anyway.

The ego problem

If you have spent any time around men in their twenties who have decided they are comedians, you will know what I mean. They are some of the most ego-maniacal people you will meet anywhere. Not in a charming, funny-confident way. In a grim way. They will tell you, unprompted, about the time they crushed at Canvas Laugh Club. They will refer to other working comics by first name as if they are personal friends. They will explain to you, in detail, why their material is too smart for the average Indian audience.

What do you actually have to be proud of? You made your dad cry when you told him you were quitting your job to be a comedian. Hats off for that. But that is the entire résumé. The set you did last week was not good. The set you did the week before was not good. The set you have planned for next week is the same set, with two new jokes that are also not going to be good. The friends who told you that you were hilarious were drunk. They are still drunk. They are also, I should mention, terrible judges of comedy.

Some of them are great salespeople. You have to give them that. The comics who reliably bring crowds, and you can spot them because they are the ones the venues actually pay, treat their seven minutes like a sales call. They have a CRM, mentally. They have a follow-up sequence. They have a conversion rate. The actual jokes are an afterthought. Their job, as they have correctly understood it, is to convert their friends and their friends' friends into a recurring weekly audience for their unfinished material, and they have got very good at that part.

You can respect a hustle without respecting the product. I do.

The clearing that never comes

But there is no other job in the world where you get rewarded for being incompetent at the actual job. Plumbers who cannot plumb get fired. Lawyers who cannot lawyer get disbarred. Software engineers who cannot ship code get put on a performance plan and then escorted out. The market eventually clears. Comedy is the one industry I have ever encountered that has structurally exempted itself from this rule, and the people inside it have decided to interpret that as evidence of how hard their job is, instead of evidence of how broken the funnel is.

And then they cry about how hard it is to be a comedian.

Shut up.

THE END.
