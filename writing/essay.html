Boil Oceans With AI but sprinkle the salt yourself

The lowest form of AI usage is also the loudest. A short argument for keeping your fingers on the keyboard while letting the machine do the dishes.

By Anant Tyagi Bombay · 6 min read

Using AI to write your essays, your captions, your thought-leader LinkedIn posts and your talking-head videos is the lowest form of AI usage. Loudest, too. Maybe second only to short-form video for net IQ-drainage per minute spent.

The thing nobody wants to say out loud: writing is not a chore you outsource. Writing is the place where thinking happens. If you outsource the writing, you've outsourced the thinking, and you didn't even charge yourself for it.

Rudimentary creation — the act of translating a fuzzy thought into a sentence that holds — should stay analog. Not analog as in pen-and-paper analog. Analog as in your fingers, your hesitation, your third rewrite of the same sentence because the second one was a lie. This is the gym. This is what stops you from dropping IQ in the four years it takes for the rest of the internet to figure out which side of the trend they're on.

So what is AI good for, then / if not the actual sentence

Boiling oceans. The unglamorous, vast, repetitive part of any creative job — the part that used to be reserved for interns and graduate students and the kind of person who'd spelunk through forty JSTOR tabs at 2am for one good citation. That's the part the machine is genuinely good at, and getting better at, and was always going to take.

You can ask it to read four hundred earnings calls. You can ask it to summarize every interview Nolan ever gave on the writing process. You can ask it to give you the seventeen counter-arguments to your draft before you publish it. None of that is the writing. All of that is data collection.

Boil oceans using AI, but sprinkle the salt yourself, not the other way round. — from this essay, said too many times now

The salt is the part that takes taste. The bit where you decide which two of the seventeen counter-arguments are real and which fifteen are noise. The bit where you cut the joke that almost works because it pulls focus from the thing that actually works. The bit where you change the rhythm of a paragraph because the cadence was off and you couldn't say why until the eighth read.

That's the part that makes the writing yours, and that's the part the machine cannot do — not because the machine isn't smart enough, but because the machine isn't you, and a sentence that wasn't sprinkled by you doesn't carry your salt. It carries the median salt of every internet writer trained on every internet thing. Median is a good place to source data. It's a terrible place to publish from.

The trade you're actually being offered

Most people who use AI to write are making a quiet trade with themselves: I'll save the time, and pay for it later in skill. Then they don't pay attention to the bill. The bill arrives in the form of a year going by where you wrote a hundred posts and didn't get any sharper at writing posts. You got better at prompting, which is a different muscle, and a less interesting one, and one that depreciates the moment the model gets better.

The reverse trade is the one worth taking. Use the machine to find the salt — the obscure stat, the dropped quote from a forty-year-old book, the pattern across six bad essays — and then sprinkle it yourself. By hand. Slowly. With your taste and your weird sentence-rhythm and your one running joke that probably isn't as funny as you think it is.

You'll write less. You'll write better. The people who already write well will keep up just fine. The people who don't will sound, increasingly, like the same midwestern customer-service email everyone else's machine generates. That divergence is going to be one of the louder features of the next five years and almost nobody is calling it that out loud.

Boil oceans with AI. Sprinkle the salt yourself. Don't get this backwards.
