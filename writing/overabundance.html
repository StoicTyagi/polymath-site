Hick's Law and the twenty-three-minute Netflix scroll

A 1952 piece of cognitive psychology has been quietly governing your decision fatigue since 2008. The fix is unfashionable.

By Anant Tyagi Bombay · 5 min read

Open Netflix. The clock starts.

You have, on the home screen, somewhere between three thousand and ten thousand things you could watch tonight. The recommendations are personalised. The trailers autoplay. There is, by any reasonable estimation, more high-quality content on this screen than your grandparents had access to in their entire lifetimes. And yet you will spend, on average, somewhere between seventeen and twenty-three minutes scrolling, before either picking something at random or giving up and rewatching an episode of a show you have already seen four times.

This phenomenon has a name. It is called Hick's Law, and a working version of it has been quietly governing your life since roughly 2008.

The 1952 paper that explains your evenings

Hick's Law, formally, is a piece of cognitive psychology from 1952. Two researchers named William Hick and Ray Hyman ran some experiments and found that the time a person takes to make a decision increases logarithmically with the number of options. Two choices, you pick fast. Four choices, slower. Sixteen choices, slower still. By the time you get to a hundred, you are not really choosing anymore. You are just freezing.

In 1952, this was a fairly academic finding, useful mostly to people designing aircraft cockpits and traffic signals. Then the internet happened, and the law turned out to be the underlying force behind almost every major piece of consumer software you now use.

Every time you have given up trying to pick a restaurant on Swiggy. Every time you have closed Spotify because you could not figure out what you were in the mood for. Every time you have stared at a Slack workspace with seventy-two channels and not joined a single one. Every time you have abandoned a wardrobe full of clothes and worn the same three things for a week. Every time you have spent forty-five minutes on Amazon trying to buy a specific kind of plug socket and ended up buying nothing. That is Hick's Law. Cognitive freeze. Menu fatigue. The sound of a prefrontal cortex giving up.

The industry's incentive to make it worse

Naval said somewhere that all the problems we have are problems of overabundance. He was right, although he was probably understating it. It is not just that the menu got too long. It is that every consumer product company in the last fifteen years has been structurally incentivised to make the menu longer. They are racing each other to add SKUs, content libraries, customisation options, recommendation engines, filters, and "you might also like" rails. Every product manager in every team meeting in every B2C company has, at some point, said the words, "What if we let the user choose?"

The user does not want to choose.

This is the thing the industry has not quite been able to admit out loud, although a few good products have figured it out and quietly built around it. Spotify Wrapped works because it is a curated annual story that you do not have to assemble. The TikTok For You page works because the user makes no choices at all, the algorithm does it for them, and the user is rewarded for sitting still. Substack works partly because email forces a serial menu of one item at a time, in chronological order, with no recommendations rail at the bottom. The best products of the last few years have all, in one way or another, been products that hide their menus.

Meanwhile, the worst products keep adding to theirs. Netflix has more rows on its homepage than ever, and a smaller share of subscribers actually using it for new content. Amazon is, depending on the category, between three and seven kinds of confusing. Indian banking apps in 2026 have so many menu items on their home screens that you can no longer find your account balance without three taps. The pattern repeats everywhere you look.

The fix nobody ships

The fix, when companies bother to apply it, is almost always the same. Reduce the number of visible options to between three and five. Make a default. Let the user override the default if they want to, but do not punish them for accepting it. This is what good restaurants do. This is what good interface designers have always done. This is what every successful chain in the world figured out forty years ago. Subway is the exception. Subway has more options than a 2025 SaaS dashboard, and Subway knows exactly what it is doing, which is making you feel busy and important while you eat a sandwich.

I should also admit that I am, in my day job, part of the problem. I work in a product category that has historically rewarded teams for adding options. Every quarter, in every business review, somebody finds a way to argue that we need to expose more of the underlying capability to our users, because surely some segment of the user base wants the granularity. Sometimes that is true. Mostly it is not. Mostly we are just adding to the menu, because nobody ever got promoted for shipping a feature that hid five existing features behind a single sane default.

So you keep your job, and the user keeps scrolling, and somewhere in a cubicle in Bangalore another product manager adds a new toggle to a settings page that nobody will ever open.

The paradox at the centre

There is a paradox at the centre of all of this and it is the only thing actually worth resolving. You cannot win by giving users no options, because then you are paternalistic and you lose the segment that wanted control. You cannot win by giving them every option, because then you lose the larger segment to Hick's Law and they leave for a simpler competitor.

The answer, almost always, is a small number of good options, presented in a clear order, with a strong default. This is not a controversial claim. It is also, somehow, the hardest thing to actually ship inside a growing company, because every team is incentivised to ship the thing that adds, not the thing that subtracts.

The next decade of consumer software is going to belong to the companies that figure out how to make the menu shorter. Not by hiding what is already there, but by being willing to delete features in service of a clearer experience. There are not many companies that can do this. Apple does it. The good open-source projects do it. Most early-stage consumer startups do it by accident, and then lose it the moment they hire their first full product team.

Everyone else is going to keep adding.

And you are going to keep scrolling.
